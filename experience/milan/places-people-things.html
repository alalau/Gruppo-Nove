Milan

Historically, Milan has long been an industrial centre, famous for manufacturing companies like Pirelli, Alfa Romeo, Lambretta and Campari, as well as for publishing. In more recent decades Milan came to be known more for fashion and design. Today it is experienced in post-industrial and monumental spaces such as the restaurant-lined Navigli district or the Corso Como, ex-Bicocca, the Piazza Vittorio Emmanuele II and the Duomo, the Vittorio Emmanuele Galleria, for museums such as the Accademia Brera and the Triennale, and the now-golbal high fashion Milanese brands such as Giorgio Armani, Prada, Trussardi and Versace. Well dressed (and not-so-well dressed by comparison to the elegant Milanese) tourists voyage here to shop on its main commercial shopping streets, extending several blocks, lined with elegant, trendy storefronts on the Via Dante and the Quadrilatoro, the Corso Buenos Aires and Via Torino. Away from the centre, and off of the city’s main attractions and streets, there’s no denying the recent economic hardships felt all over Italy. The city is painted in expressive graffiti, a visual impression of the city’s vocal and subversive youth culture. Small protests can burst anywhere, stopping local traffic. Caught in the intersection between luxury and the working class left from it’s industrial past, and a constant influx of legal and illegal immigration, Milan is working to find its future and the place of design in a changing world.
